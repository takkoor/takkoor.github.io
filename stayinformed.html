Designing for Media Literacy

A visual guide to recognizing misinformation, disinformation, and propaganda online

Context

Users encounter misleading information daily across social platforms, often without the tools to distinguish between error, manipulation, and intent.

Problem

Media literacy concepts are frequently taught abstractly, making them difficult to recognize in fast-moving, real-world digital environments.

Audience

  • Students
  • General internet users
  • Educators teaching media literacy

Constraints

  • Had to communicate quickly and visually
  • No interactivity available
  • Needed to work across different literacy levels
  • Had to avoid moralizing or shaming language

Design Goals

  • Clearly distinguish misinformation, disinformation, and propaganda
  • Use real-world visual cues users recognize
  • Encourage critical thinking without overwhelming users

Key Design Decisions

Why strong color contrast?

High contrast separates concepts clearly and reflects the urgency of navigating high-noise information environments.

Why numbered sections?

A linear structure supports scanning and quick comprehension, allowing users to internalize concepts incrementally.

Why platform-specific visuals?

Familiar interfaces help users transfer learning directly to their daily digital experiences.

Why mix examples with statistics?

Concrete examples build intuition, while data reinforces scale and impact.

Outcome & Impact

  • Made abstract concepts tangible and memorable
  • Supported classroom and self-guided learning
  • Encouraged skepticism without cynicism

Reflection

This work could extend into an interactive learning experience or browser-based intervention that supports real-time information evaluation.
