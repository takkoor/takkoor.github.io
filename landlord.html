Rate Your Landlord

Designing for transparency and trust in student housing decisions

Project type: Group project
My role: Product design, user research, interaction design
Team: Collaborative student design team
Tools: Figma

Context

Student renters often sign leases with limited information about landlords, property conditions, or tenant experiences. Existing review platforms are fragmented, difficult to trust, or poorly suited to the student housing market.

This project explored how a mobile-first product could support more informed housing decisions by making landlord experiences easier to share, evaluate, and compare.

Problem

How might we help student renters make higher-confidence housing decisions in a system where power and information are unevenly distributed?

Audience

  • College students searching for off-campus housing
  • First-time renters unfamiliar with leasing risks
  • Student communities reliant on peer-to-peer information

Constraints

  • Students need to make decisions quickly during leasing cycles
  • Trust and credibility are critical but difficult to establish
  • Content must be scannable on mobile devices
  • As a concept project, no live data or moderation system existed

Design Goals

  • Increase transparency without exposing users to retaliation
  • Support quick comparison between landlords and properties
  • Encourage contributions while minimizing friction
  • Design for trust in a peer-generated information system

Key Design Decisions

Mobile-first layout

Students often search for housing on their phones. Prioritizing mobile ensured reviews and ratings were scannable and actionable in real contexts.

Structured review prompts

Instead of open-ended reviews, structured prompts helped users share relevant, comparable information while reducing cognitive load.

Emphasis on patterns over single ratings

The interface highlighted recurring issues (e.g., maintenance, communication) to help users avoid over-weighting individual anecdotes.

Privacy-conscious contribution flow

The design avoided unnecessary personal identifiers to reduce fear of landlord retaliation, encouraging more honest participation.

Prototype & Artifacts

Outcome

  • Produced a mid-fidelity prototype demonstrating core user flows
  • Clarified how design can redistribute information power in housing markets
  • Strengthened my experience collaborating in a group design setting

Reflection

If taken further, this concept would require robust moderation, verification mechanisms, and careful incentive design to balance openness with safety. This project reinforced how trust, not just usability, is central to marketplace design.
